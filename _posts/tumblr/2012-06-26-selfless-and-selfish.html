Finished reading The Fountainhead by Ayn Rand and i am just blown away. The central idea and theme of the book is very strong, interesting and revealing (imo). I had been thinking much about it and things like these always concerned me. Things like : “There is no such thing as free will”.

How much portion of our lives do we truly own? I try experimenting and challenging and pushing the boundaries to get a sense of it. Are my decisions, my own? How much influence do people have on the course of my life and is it desirable? How can i prevent it? How can i become truly self sufficient? What things do i need to do?  What things do i want to do? There is an important distinction between ‘need’ and 'want’.

I need to pay my tax. There doesn’t seems to be a question of 'want’ here; it’s a rule. What does it do? It gives me the freedom to be 'happy’. That is my 'want’. So is that a compromise? No. Because when you understand that doing this thing, in turn, gives access to your other 'wants’, you then start wanting it. And if otherwise, that need has absolutely no relation with your want, then it’s irrelevant. 

Lets briefly review our social lives here. What makes us 'happy’? Getting 'Likes’ or more 'Followers’ or more 'Karma’? Do we live our life for others? That’s so goddamn selfless. Congratulations! We all are public property(!?). Some time ago i saw, a movie, about a young guy, who abandons his 'career’ and family, becomes a hitchhiker; discovering and experiencing new things, is what he 'wants’ and 'likes’ to do and that’s what he does. In the end of his solitary journey, he concludes something like this: Any accomplishment is not worthy if it can’t be shared or if there isn’t someone to appreciate it, because real happiness comes from that. 

 Ayn Rand’s hero argues that true happiness should come only from the sole existence of the accomplishment at any point of time. So it’s like a (public)blog post with zero page views. In this case, it is the result of your creative instincts, and that you wrote it because you wanted to; knowing that it existed at some point of time should be the real cause of 'happiness’. Only then you are a 'selfish’ person and not a 'second-hander’.

For some context : Howard Roark’s(the protagonist’s) final speech.
