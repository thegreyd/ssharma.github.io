Once in a year Google holds its IO event, and unveils its grand future plans for its products. This year was a special one, with Google launching two brand new hardware products, Nexus 7 and Nexus Q , and giving a stunning preview of its futuristic and nerdiest project yet –> Google Glass.

Who knew Google could do hardware? Well, the tablet wasn’t a surprise, but impressive for sure. I was really waiting for it, and am keen on buying it. Nexus Q looks cool; not much otherwise.

Onto software - Google+, looks more enticing, now with beautiful mobile apps and new Events feature. Android Jellybean introduced, with Project Butter and Google Now. Project Butter was so sorely needed. Google Now is a platform to harness Google’s search prowess, equipped with its knowledge graph, along with voice recognition; taking forward the Siri-like functionality(without the jibber-jabber). So the major arguments in the android vs ios debate have been addressed; android has matured. 

One of the interesting things to me was the absence of standard tablet ui on Nexus 7 and the phone ui instead(with tablet apps). The decision was smart. It is perfectly usable on a 7 inch screen. But what about 10 inch ones? Well, Google advocates the 7 inch being the perfect tablet size, and i fully agree, so never mind. 

UPDATE: Here’s Diana Hackborn’s(Android engineer) take on it : https://plus.google.com/105051985738280261832/posts/6eWwQvFGLV8 

Google Glass’s first live demo was a bunch of sky-divers in a G+ hangout jumping on the roof of the conference building. Then came the bikers. It was amazing ,and thrilling!  The prototype version was on pre-order for the attendees, and by 2014 Glass is expected to go for public consumption.

So its fairly evident, that photos and videos are gonna be among the main focuses of Glass. Other than that, we haven’t seen anything interesting. It is said to handle text, emails and much more. As for input, there is a touchpad on the right and eye and head motion controls, like blinking and head-tilting. I assume glass is primarily controlled by the eye, but how, i don’t know. And what’s the ui like? Sergey said it is hard to demonstrate someone operating Glass (for obvious reasons), so i think i would have to suffice with the experiences of tech bloggers having a chance at it.
